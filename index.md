# Zeev Goldschmidt

I am a postdoctoral fellow at the <a href="https://edelstein.huji.ac.il/">Edelstein Center for Philosophy of Science</a> at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem. I received my PhD in Philosophy from the <a href="https://www.lse.ac.uk/philosophy">London School of Economics</a>. I work primarily in epistemology, decision theory, and ethics. Broadly characterized, my research focuses on the relationships between agents’ mental states and their actions. This includes topics like the role knowledge has to play in decision theory, what moral uncertainty implies for action, and how attitudes to risk shape a rational agent’s preferences.

My email is zeev.goldschmidt [at] huji.ac.il

## Publications

<details style="margin-bottom: 12px;">
  <summary>
    <span style="color: #0366d6; font-size: 0.8em; margin-right: 10px;">▶</span>
    <strong>Moral Uncertainty Resolution as Belief Binarization: An Impossibility Result</strong>&nbsp;with&nbsp;<a href="https://personal.lse.ac.uk/list/">Christian List</a>, forthcoming in <em>Economics and Philosophy</em>. <a href="https://philpapers.org/archive/GOLMUR.pdf" style="margin-left: 5px; color: #0366d6;">[Preprint]</a>
  </summary>
  <div style="margin-top: 5px; border-left: 2px solid #333; padding-left: 15px; font-size: 0.95em;">
    Coming up with a rule to guide a morally-uncertain agent is tricky in ways similar to coming up with a rule governing the relationship between credences and full beliefs. This similarity allows us to apply an impossibility theorem about belief binarization to the problem of moral uncertainty, thereby mapping the logical space of necessarily imperfect solutions to this problem.
  </div>
</details>

<details style="margin-bottom: 12px;">
  <summary>
    <span style="color: #0366d6; font-size: 0.8em; margin-right: 10px;">▶</span>
    <strong><a href="https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/epdf/10.1080/00048402.2024.2328635?needAccess=true">Foundations for Knowledge-Based Decision Theories</a></strong>, <em>Australasian Journal of Philosophy</em>, 2024
  </summary>
  <div style="margin-top: 5px; border-left: 2px solid #333; padding-left: 15px; font-size: 0.95em;">
    How could decision theory incorporate the idea that we ought to act on what we know? I propose a framework for formulating and justifying Knowledge-Based Decision Theories (KDTs), and I prove a representation theorem for a frequently discussed KDT.
  </div>
</details>

<details style="margin-bottom: 12px;">
  <summary>
    <span style="color: #0366d6; font-size: 0.8em; margin-right: 10px;">▶</span>
    <strong><a href="https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-020-02532-3">The Intrinsic Value of Risky Prospects</a></strong>, <em>Synthese</em>, 2020 <a href="https://drive.google.com/file/d/1ztX4MLByOspHThBH66YKUiDVFWgS_7hy/view?usp=sharing" style="margin-left: 5px; color: #0366d6;">[Preprint]</a>
  </summary>
  <div style="margin-top: 5px; border-left: 2px solid #333; padding-left: 15px; font-size: 0.95em;">
    A rational agent may value a risky prospect instrumentally – in virtue of the values of the outcomes it may bring about – or intrinsically – in virtue of its purely probabilistic features. We demonstrate that the value of a risky prospect for a rational agent is the sum of its expected instrumental value and its expected intrinsic value.
  </div>
</details>
