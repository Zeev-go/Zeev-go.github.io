# Zeev Goldschmidt


<!-- [Profile Picture](images/profile.jpg){: .align-left width="200px"} -->


I am a postdoctoral fellow at the [Edelstein Center for Philosophy of Science](https://edelstein.huji.ac.il/){:target="_blank"} at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem. I received my PhD in Philosophy from the <a href="https://www.lse.ac.uk/philosophy" target="_blank" style="color: inherit; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 1px dotted #ccc;">London School of Economics</a>. 
I work primarily in epistemology, decision theory, and ethics. Broadly characterized, my research focuses on the relationships between agents’ mental states and their actions. This includes topics like the role knowledge has to play in decision theory, what moral uncertainty implies for action, and how attitudes to risk shape a rational agent’s preferences. 

My email is zeev.goldschmidt [at] .ac.il

## Publications

<details style="margin-bottom: 12px; list-style: none;">
  <summary style="cursor: pointer; list-style: none; outline: none; display: block;">
    <span style="color: #0366d6; font-size: 0.8em; vertical-align: middle;">▶</span> 
    <strong>Moral Uncertainty Resolution as Belief Binarization: An Impossibility Result</strong> 
    with <a href="https://personal.lse.ac.uk/list/" target="_blank" style="color: inherit; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 1px dotted #ccc;">Christian List</a>, 
    forthcoming in <em>Economics and Philosophy</em>. 
    <a href="https://philpapers.org/archive/GOLMUR.pdf" target="_blank" style="text-decoration: none; font-size: 0.9em; color: #0366d6;">[Preprint]</a>
  </summary>
  <div style="margin-left: 0px; margin-top: 5px; border-left: 2px solid #333; padding-left: 15px; color: ; font-size: 0.95em;">
    Coming up with a rule to guide a morally-uncertain agent is tricky in ways similar to coming up with a rule governing the relationship between credences and full beliefs. This similarity allows us to apply an impossibility theorem about belief binarization to the problem of moral uncertainty, thereby mapping the logical space of necessarily imperfect solutions to this problem.
  </div>
</details>


<details style="margin-bottom: 12px; list-style: none;">
  <summary style="cursor: pointer; list-style: none; outline: none; display: block;">
    <span style="color: #0366d6; font-size: 0.8em; vertical-align: middle;">▶</span> 
    <strong><a href="https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/epdf/10.1080/00048402.2024.2328635?needAccess=true" target="_blank" style="color: inherit; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 1px dotted #ccc;">Foundations for Knowledge-Based Decision Theories</a></strong>, 
    <em>Australasian Journal of Philosophy</em>, 2024 
    <a href="link-to-pdf.pdf" target="_blank" style="text-decoration: none; font-size: 0.9em; color: #0366d6;"></a>
  </summary>
  <div style="margin-left: 0px; margin-top: 5px; border-left: 2px solid #333; padding-left: 15px; color: ; font-size: 0.95em;">
    How could decision theory incorporate the idea that we ought to act on what we know? I propose a framework for formulating and justifying Knowledge-Based Decision Theories (KDTs), and I prove a representation theorem for a frequently discussed KDT.
  </div>
</details>

<details style="margin-bottom: 12px; list-style: none;">
  <summary style="cursor: pointer; list-style: none; outline: none; display: block;">
    <span style="color: #0366d6; font-size: 0.8em; vertical-align: middle;">▶</span> 
    <strong><a href="https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11229-020-02532-3" target="_blank" style="color: inherit; text-decoration: none; border-bottom: 1px dotted #ccc;">The Intrinsic Value of Risky Prospects</a></strong>, 
    <em>Synthese</em>, 2020 
    <a href="https://drive.google.com/file/d/1ztX4MLByOspHThBH66YKUiDVFWgS_7hy/view?usp=sharing" target="_blank" style="text-decoration: none; font-size: 0.9em; color: #0366d6;">[Preprint]</a>
  </summary>
  <div style="margin-left: 0px; margin-top: 5px; border-left: 2px solid #333; padding-left: 15px; color: ; font-size: 0.95em;">
    A rational agent may value a risky prospect instrumentally – in virtue of the values of the outcomes it may bring about – or intrinsically – in virtue of its purely probabilistic features. We demonstrate that the value of a risky prospect for a rational agent is the sum of its expected instrumental value and its expected intrinsic value.
  </div>
</details>

  <summary style="cursor: pointer; list-style: none; outline: none; display: block;">
    <span style="color: #0366d6; font-size: 0.8em; vertical-align: middle;">▶</span> 
    <strong>Three Equivalent Approaches to Moral Uncertainty – A Consolidation Theorem</strong> 
    <a href="link-to-pdf.pdf" target="_blank" style="text-decoration: none; font-size: 0.9em; color: #0366d6;"></a>
  </summary>
  <div style="margin-left: 0px; margin-top: 5px; border-left: 2px solid #333; padding-left: 15px; color: ; font-size: 0.95em;">
    I consider three approaches to the problem of moral uncertainty where comparisons of moral value across theories are indeterminate: Non-Domination, Expected Value Maximization, and Nash Bargaining. I demonstrate that the three approaches are, in important ways, equivalent, and face similar arbitrariness objections. The result demonstrates the stubbornness of the problem of indeterminacy and the limited utility of applying the various formal approaches to solving it.
  </div>
</details>
